# Cartography: progress in tactile mapping

## One-Sentence Summary
A narrative progress report (first of three on cartography) that synthesizes tactile-mapping research for visually impaired people published since Fleming (1990) across six thematic strands — cognitive research, design, standardization, production technology, technological substitutes/enhancements, and ethics — arguing that real-world uptake is limited and that the research community should re-centre on the social context of map use rather than chasing high-technology solutions. *(p.521)*

## Problem Addressed
Tactile mapping is treated as marginal to mainstream cartography; production is informal, receives no attention in the latest benchmark world-mapping survey (Parry and Perkins, 2000), and most research is laboratory-based, western, urban, positivist, sighted-researcher-led, and disconnected from users' lived experience — so despite decades of work, most blind people do not use the technologies produced. *(p.521, p.525-526)*

## Key Contributions
- Structures the post-Fleming (1990) literature into six themes that mirror mainstream cartographic research concerns: cognition & map use, design, standardization, production technology, dispensing with / enhancing the map (technological fixes), and ethics. *(p.521)*
- Compiles a substantial reference base (~70 citations) covering laboratory cognitive work, real-world wayfinding studies, national production surveys, symbol standards (Nottingham kit, Euro Town Kit, TIGER, NCTD, ICEB Tactile Graphics Project), production families (multitextural, etching, embossing, vacuum-forming), GIS-driven production, audio-tactile systems (NOMAD, TACIS, Talking Tactile Tablet), personal guidance systems (MOBIC, Atlas Speaks/Strider, Santa Barbara PGS, Auditory Location Finder), and VR work. *(p.522-525, p.526-530 references)*
- Frames a critique: research is largely sighted-led, urban, western, academy-based, positivist, and based on an inappropriate medical model of disability (Gleeson, 1999); recommends a shift toward participatory research, low-tech approaches in developing-world contexts, and letting social context drive design decisions. *(p.525-526)*
- Provides Gardiner's (2001) four-family taxonomy of production technologies as the de facto framing of the field. *(p.524)*
- Surfaces the key empirical claim from Gill (1999): despite 25+ years of electronic mobility aid development, "none have proved popular with more than a handful of users." *(p.525)*

## Detailed Thematic Extraction

### I. Cognitive research and map use *(p.521-522)*
- Dominant 1990s research focus: psychological investigations of spatial representation (Millar, 1994) and wayfinding abilities (Golledge, 1999) in visually impaired children and adults. Review coverage: Kitchin et al. 1997; Ungar 2000; Jansson 2000. *(p.521)*
- Most work is laboratory-based, studying small-scale and artificial environments. Consensus: blind people have the same cognitive spatial abilities as sighted people, but configurational knowledge is less complex due to differing access to information and past experience (Golledge, 1993). *(p.521)*
- Claim: use of tactile mapping may significantly improve VI people's cognitive maps and help wayfinding / environmental knowledge (Blades et al., 2000). *(p.521)*
- Shift toward larger-scale and real-world environments (Golledge et al., 1999; Kitchin et al., 1998); role of tactile maps in understanding them (Ungar et al., 1994). *(p.522)*
- Specific child-focused studies: distance estimation from maps (Ungar et al., 1994, 1997a), map-memorizing strategies (Ungar et al., 1995a), symbol layout & alignment recall (Ungar et al., 1995b), self-location (Ungar et al., 1996), role of mapping in informing route knowledge (Edwards et al., 1998). *(p.522)*
- Real-world adult wayfinding and map-reading strategies: route-learning comparisons between direct experience and tactile mapping in Sheffield and Madrid (Espinosa et al., 1998; Ungar et al., 1998); documentation of spontaneous strategies (Ungar et al., 1997b); wider context (Blades et al., 2000). *(p.522)*
- Other: use before vs during travel (Bringhammar et al., 1997); different ways of assessing cognitive map knowledge (Jacobson, 1998). *(p.522)*
- Educational justification thread: better cognitive-issue knowledge → better mobility training during school years (Ungar et al., 1993; Blades et al., 2000). *(p.522)*

### II. Map design *(p.522-523)*
- Continuing emphasis on different designs, contexts, uses; state-of-the-art national overviews: Tanzania (Mtaroni, 1999), India (Kumar, 1999), Japan (Ohtsuka et al., 1997), Slovenia (Rener, 1996), Argentina (Amenguel and Cuppi, 1997), plus western European, North American, Australasian research. *(p.522)*
- Production context varies by nation, almost always significantly different from conventional mapping practice (Perkins and Parry, 1996 for the UK). Characterized by limited print runs, strong influence of national blind charities, no conventional market mechanism, no "commercial" production. *(p.522)*
- "Enabling" legislation and mobility-training infrastructure are powerful influences (Blasch et al., 1997 as definitive guide for the US context). *(p.522)*
- Tactile mapping is more likely to be available for public urban space and focused on mobility. Example domains researched: university campuses (Golledge et al., 1991; Hudson, 1998; Jacobson, 1992; Koch and Liebmann, 1995; Perkins, 1999); shopping centres (Tatham, 1990); public-transport mapping (Landau, 1999; Luxton et al., 1994). *(p.522)*
- Recent extension: indoor mobility maps; path- vs wall-based symbology research (Holmes and Arditi, 1998). *(p.522)*
- Route-based vs area-based mapping requirements noted (Golledge, 1991). *(p.523)*
- Thematic tactile mapping is under-researched; Blok (1993) cited as a rare paper. Recent papers extend tactile mapping to orienteering (Gardiner, 1998b), canal towpaths (Gardiner and Perkins, 1996a), golf courses (Gardiner and Perkins, 1996b), country parks (Gardiner, 1997). Reference-map design for education explored by Wild et al. (1997); environmental-education tactile maps (Gardiner, 1998a). *(p.523)*

### III. Standardization *(p.523)*
- Two strands, neither yet producing international agreement equivalent to the digital-database standards used by sighted-user cartography. *(p.523)*
- **Strand 1 — standardized tactile symbols linked to a production technology:** long history from University of Nottingham (James and Armstrong, 1976). Nottingham kit widely used in urban contexts. Enhanced in 1988 by Europe-wide acceptance of the Euro Town Kit: 28 tactile symbols for urban tactile mapping (Laufenberg, 1988). Other de facto standardization via acceptance of standard design works (Edman, 1992). *(p.523)*
- Recent exploration: Tatham (2001) on progress; plans for an international Database of Tactile Symbols, web-disseminated, covering dimensions, context of use, and recommended production technology per symbol (Tasker, 2000). *(p.523)*
- **Strand 2 — presentation/design-level standardization:** Law (1988) on codifying good practice in map design rather than symbols. Tatham (1999) argues guidelines must be independent of production technology because resources and infrastructure vary greatly. *(p.523)*
- National guidelines published by American Printing House (1997) and American National Library Service for the Blind (Corcoran, 2000). *(p.523)*
- Institutional: National Centre for Tactile Diagrams (NCTD) established 1999 at University of Hertfordshire, advising designers (NCTD, 2001); Tactile Graphics Project established 2000 by the International Council on English Braille to coordinate international tactile-graphics research and write production guidelines (ICEB, 2000). *(p.523)*
- The debate on standards is ongoing. *(p.523)*

### IV. Production technology *(p.524)*
- Production technology profoundly affects use and is closely linked to symbol design. *(p.524)*
- Gardiner (2001) four-family taxonomy of hard-copy production:
  1. multitextural
  2. etching
  3. embossing (hand, machine, and print based)
  4. vacuum-forming
- **Current dominant modality:** vacuum-forming and microcapsule / foam-ink systems still produce the vast majority of hard-copy tactile graphics despite electronic mobility aid progress. *(p.524)*
- Few publications in the last decade on these technologies; little systematic comparative evaluation (Perkins, 2001 for limited review). *(p.524)*
- Contradictory preference results: Nagel and Coulson (1990) found significant preferences for microcapsule paper; Gardiner (2001) reports multilevel graphics in thermoform offering significant advantages. Context and design of trials drive the divergence. *(p.524)*
- Thermoform mass-production bottleneck: time to create master moulds. Dahlberg (1997) explores machine routing of masters in conjunction with a GIS application; Gardner (1996) reviews American progress. *(p.524)*
- Merging braille with raised graphics is another core difficulty. Two solutions:
  - Lange and Reeves (1999): new type of inkjet printer developed under EU funding with commercial vendor (PRINT project), depositing raised dots and graphics on the same page. *(p.524)*
  - TIGER system: Windows-based graphics embosser, commercially available through Viewplus Technologies (Walsh and Gardner, 2001). *(p.524)*
- 2001 investigations: systems generating multilevel PVC-gel symbols via computer-driven printing; CAD-driven wax printing; printers capable of raising microcapsule linework in a single pass (Gardiner, 2001). *(p.524)*
- "Innovation is likely to continue." *(p.524)*

### V. Dispensing with, or enhancing, the map: technological fixes *(p.524-525)*
- Core challenge: lack of appropriate map data and the need to generalize existing digital data to produce discriminable tactile symbols (Tatham, 1991). *(p.524)*
- Early 1990s: drawing/mapping software became widely used for tactile-map masters (Theissen, 1999); first attempts to generate appropriate symbols from GIS (Coulson, 1991). *(p.524)*
- Automatic derivation of tactile mapping from digital map data:
  - Sheppard (1996) on Ordnance Survey data
  - Ohtsuka et al. (1997) on Japanese Geographical Survey Institute data
  - Michel (1997) on German ALK data *(p.524)*
- GIS to support mobility: Jacobson and Kitchin (1997); Clark (1997); Golledge et al. (1991). *(p.524)*
- **Personal Guidance Systems (PGS):** combine GIS + digital mapping + GPS locating + multimedia user interfaces (synthesized voice, virtual auditory displays, tactile route output) (Jacobson, 1994). *(p.524-525)*
- Pilot PGS work: Santa Barbara campus (Golledge et al., 1998; Loomis et al., 2001). *(p.525)*
- Commercial / other guidance systems: Atlas Speaks and Strider (Arkenstone, mid-1990s; Lapierre, 1998; currently via Sendeco — May, 2001). *(p.525)*
- European mid-1990s: MOBIC project — GPS+GIS with auditory output, pre-journey and outdoor systems (MOBIC Consortium, 1997). *(p.525)*
- Non-GPS alternative: radio-beacon local information systems, e.g., Auditory Location Finder (Hine et al., 2000). *(p.525)*
- **Touch-tablet sound+tactile integration (no navigation):** NOMAD (Parkes, 1988) as the first such system; numerous NOMAD maps developed globally (Hudson, 1998). *(p.525)*
- TACIS project: touch, tone landscapes, voice overlays (Lange, 1999); multimedia information system for blind people in European cities with Dublin pilot (Gallagher and Frasch, 1997). *(p.525)*
- Talking kiosks operable in the USA, similar touch+audio combinations (Landau, 1999). *(p.525)*
- VR directions:
  - Multimedia + VR for mobility skills teaching (Krueger and Gilden, 1999; Jacobson and Kitchin, 2002)
  - VR for improving conventional tactile-map design (Cheesman and Perkins, 2002) *(p.525)*

### VI. Ethics *(p.525-526)*
- John Gill (RNIB Chief Scientist, 1999): despite 25 years of electronic mobility aid development, "none have proved popular with more than a handful of users." Most blind people do not use them. Most are unaware of the potential of simpler traditional hard-copy tactile maps. *(p.525)*
- The ethics and practice of research into tactile mapping can explain this lack of real-world application (Perkins and Gardiner, 1997). *(p.525)*
- Problematic research sociology:
  - Almost all tactile-mapping research is carried out by sighted people. *(p.525)*
  - Largely western and urban context. *(p.525)*
  - Mostly in the academy, not in communities. *(p.525)*
  - Quality judged by academic criteria, not contribution to quality of life. *(p.525)*
- Critique (Gleeson, 1999): the emphasis is implicitly positivist and technocratic, based on an inappropriate medical model of disability, ignoring the wider social constructions of visual impairment. *(p.525)*
- Positionality of the researcher has been problematized; call to give greater emphasis to the blind subject's view of the world (Butler and Bowlby, 1997; Kitchin, 1999). *(p.526)*
- Recent work discussing with VI people how they "see" the world: Golledge, 1997; Johnson and Petrie, 1998; Kitchin et al., 1998; Pow, 2000. *(p.526)*
- Participatory design approaches: Perkins and Gardiner, 1997; Gardiner, 2001. Bottom-up mobility-mapping: Matthews and Vujakovic, 1995. *(p.526)*
- Most reported initiatives are from the developed world; VI people in villages in developing countries are ill-served (Mtaroni, 1999). *(p.526)*
- Recommendation for poor / third-world contexts: use whatever materials are available, rather than impose western design standardization or technology (Tatham, 1999). *(p.526)*
- **Closing recommendation (Gardiner, 2001 via Perkins):** researchers should focus more on the social context of map use and let that drive design decisions, instead of spending large research grants on often-inappropriate technological solutions. *(p.526)*

## Results Summary
No "results" in the empirical sense. The synthesis concludes that:
- The tactile-mapping research field mirrors mainstream cartographic research in its thematic concerns but lags in standardization, uptake, and inclusivity. *(p.521, 523)*
- Hard-copy vacuum-forming and microcapsule production still dominate practical output despite decades of electronic mobility-aid research. *(p.524-525)*
- No international standardization equivalent to sighted-user digital map databases has emerged. *(p.523)*
- Despite technological proliferation (NOMAD, TACIS, MOBIC, PGS, TIGER, VR), user uptake is negligible. *(p.525)*
- The underlying failure is ethical/methodological: sighted-led, positivist, academy-based, western-urban, medical-model research. *(p.525-526)*
- A redirection toward participatory research, social-context-driven design, and low-tech solutions in developing-world contexts is advocated. *(p.526)*

## Arguments Against Prior Work
- **Against laboratory-only cognitive studies:** "Most of the work carried out has been laboratory-based and has investigated small-scale and artificial environments." Calls for shift to larger-scale real-world environments. *(p.521-522)*
- **Against the high-technology trajectory:** Gill (1999): 25 years of electronic mobility aids have produced essentially no popular systems. *(p.525)*
- **Against sighted-led, urban, western, academy-centric research:** Gleeson (1999) framing taken up — positivist, technocratic, medical-model, ignores social constructions of visual impairment. *(p.525)*
- **Against assuming VI users' lack of awareness is a minor issue:** "most are not even aware of the potential of simpler and more traditional hard-copy tactile maps" — implying both a research-communication failure and an inappropriate research focus. *(p.525)*
- **Against western design-standardization imposition in developing contexts:** Tatham (1999) and the developing-world critique — "impose western notions of design standardization or technology" is rejected. *(p.526)*
- **Against inappropriate research spending:** "spending large research grants on often inappropriate technological solutions." *(p.526)*
- **Against Parry and Perkins (2000) — self-critique:** The latest benchmark world-mapping survey (co-authored by this paper's author) gives no attention to tactile production, which is cited as evidence of how marginalized the field is. *(p.521)*

## Design Rationale
- **Why six themes?** Perkins argues the chosen themes "reflect current concerns in the wider worlds of cartographic research" — i.e., tactile mapping is being framed as continuous with mainstream cartography, not a niche accessibility subdomain. *(p.521)*
- **Why hard-copy still dominates:** closely linked to symbol design; production technology changes slowly; commercial market mechanisms are absent; national blind charities drive production. *(p.522, 524)*
- **Why standards are hard:** variation in national contexts, production technology, and resources; Tatham's (1999) argument that design guidelines must be independent of production technology. *(p.523)*
- **Why dispensing-with-map technologies fail in practice:** despite integrated GIS+GPS+audio+tactile+VR systems, none are popular. The paper implicitly attributes this to research culture rather than engineering limits. *(p.525)*
- **Why ethics is treated as the final theme:** the paper builds up the technical literature and then delivers the critical turn, arguing the technical agenda itself is the problem. *(p.525-526)*

## Testable Properties
- **Property 1:** Electronic mobility aids developed 1974-1999 have negligible real-world usage (Gill 1999 claim). Testable via contemporary user surveys. *(p.525)*
- **Property 2:** Hard-copy production is dominated by vacuum-forming + microcapsule/foam-ink. Testable by surveying current tactile-map production facilities. *(p.524)*
- **Property 3:** Preferences between microcapsule paper and multilevel thermoform differ by trial context (Nagel & Coulson 1990 vs. Gardiner 2001). Testable via replication with controlled task contexts. *(p.524)*
- **Property 4:** Blind and sighted users have equivalent cognitive spatial abilities but differing configurational-knowledge complexity (Golledge 1993). Testable via standardized spatial-cognition batteries. *(p.521)*
- **Property 5:** Tactile map use improves VI users' cognitive maps and wayfinding ability (Blades et al. 2000 claim). Testable via pre/post wayfinding RCTs. *(p.521)*
- **Property 6:** The 28-symbol Euro Town Kit (Laufenberg 1988) is Europe-wide accepted. Testable via document review of European tactile-map production. *(p.523)*
- **Property 7:** Research published in this field is predominantly sighted-author, western-urban, academy-based. Testable via bibliometric analysis of author affiliations and positionality statements. *(p.525)*

## Relevance to Project
This is a foundational reference for the inclusive-cartography project for three reasons:

1. **Framing legitimacy.** Perkins (2002) is cited repeatedly by downstream tactile-cartography research (including the Wabiński et al. 2022 guidelines work and every systematic review of tactile/inclusive maps since). Any inclusive-cartography protocol that does not acknowledge Perkins' six-theme framing is out of step with the canonical literature.

2. **Ethical/methodological gate.** Perkins' Section VI (Ethics) directly anticipates the kinds of critique that should be applied to any LLM-confidence-threshold protocol in this project. If the Wabiński protocol's thresholds are calibrated against sighted-author, western-urban, academy-based papers — Perkins' critique applies directly. The §3.3 seed-selection debate in the second batch should explicitly engage with whether the confidence thresholds reproduce the same positivist/technocratic bias that Perkins indicts.

3. **Historical baseline.** The claim that hard-copy vacuum-forming + microcapsule still dominate despite 25+ years of high-tech alternatives is a durable benchmark against which any "new technology solves tactile mapping" paper must be evaluated. Most novelty claims in the later literature (3D printing, AR tactile overlays, haptic tablets) should be assessed against this baseline.
