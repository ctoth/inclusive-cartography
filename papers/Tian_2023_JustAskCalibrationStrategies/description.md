Tian et al. evaluate "just ask" strategies — verbalized numeric probabilities, linguistic likelihood expressions, and top-k with per-answer confidences — for eliciting calibrated confidence from RLHF-fine-tuned language models (GPT-4, ChatGPT, Claude-1/2, Llama-2-70B-chat) on TriviaQA, SciQ, and TruthfulQA. They find that verbalized probabilities, particularly in a two-step protocol, achieve roughly 50% lower Expected Calibration Error than the models' own conditional token probabilities or P(True) self-evaluation, requiring no fine-tuning, labeled calibration data, or logit access. The result is directly relevant to this project's critique of Wabinski 2026's LLM-confidence-threshold screening rule: it bounds how good and how bad verbalized confidences from RLHF chatbots can be, and supplies concrete prompt-level fixes (top-k, two-step elicitation) that Wabinski's protocol could adopt.
