## Key Citations for Follow-up

- **Kadavath et al. 2022 — "Language models (mostly) know what they know"** (Anthropic). Directly parallel investigation into whether LMs have self-knowledge, from the probing side rather than the finetuning side. Strong candidate for the Wabinski 2026 critique because it provides scale / task diversity that Lin 2022 lacks, and because its P(IK) methodology is itself a precursor to modern verbalized-confidence prompts.
- **Mielke et al. 2022 — "Reducing conversational agents' overconfidence through linguistic calibration"** (TACL). Closest prior art on teaching models to hedge in natural language for a non-math task; provides evidence on open-ended dialog settings.
- **Jiang, Araki, Ding & Neubig 2021 — "How can we know when language models know?"** (TACL). The probe-the-logits baseline method Lin 2022 contrasts against, evaluated on QA. Useful for understanding the failure modes of in-distribution logit calibration that motivate verbalized confidence.
- **Guo, Pleiss, Sun & Weinberger 2017 — "On calibration of modern neural networks"** (ICML). Canonical reference establishing that modern networks are overconfident and temperature scaling fixes in-distribution but not OOD calibration. This is the implicit target of Lin 2022's OOD comparisons.
- **Ovadia et al. 2019 — "Can You Trust Your Model's Uncertainty?"** (NeurIPS). Systematic study of calibration degradation under dataset shift; methodological ancestor of Lin 2022's Add/Sub → Multi-answer / Multiply-divide shift framing.
