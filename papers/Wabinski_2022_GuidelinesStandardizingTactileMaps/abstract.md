# Abstract

## Original Text (Verbatim)

Accessibility to tactile maps is limited due to their expensive and time-consuming development. Acceleration of their production requires standardized design guidelines that consider symbol design and production methods. In this paper, based on a review of research and best practice, we summarize knowledge on how to design tactile maps properly and provide a selection of highly legible, recommended symbols for the compilation of tactile maps. We also examine generalization constraints and other design parameters that are necessary for the standardization of tactile mapping. Finally, we explore differences in tactile map design depending upon the selected production method. Over the years, many useful guidelines have been developed although they remain unknown to the wider audience. There is still a long way to go in creating a global standard for the design of tactile maps.

## Our Interpretation

Wabinski, Moscicka and Touya synthesise scattered empirical and practitioner literature into a single reference for tactile-map design, focused on four axes: symbol inventory (what shapes, at what sizes), generalization (how to reduce a source map into a tactile map without losing meaning), measurable design parameters (dimensions, distances, relief heights), and production-method sensitivity (thermoform vs swell-paper vs braille embosser vs 3D print). Their central operational artifacts are the consolidated parameter tables (Tables 2-4, p.12) and the Appendix A symbol catalogue (pp.18-20), which together give a practitioner numeric lower bounds and a ready-to-use symbol set traceable to empirical distinguishability studies. For the inclusive-cartography project this paper is directly load-bearing: it supplies the numeric invariants an automated tactile-map pipeline must respect and flags the gap — absence of a global standard — that motivates the 2026 systematic-review protocol by the same lead author.
