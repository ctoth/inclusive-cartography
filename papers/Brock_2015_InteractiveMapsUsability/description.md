Brock et al. present the first controlled within-subjects usability comparison of a classical braille-legend raised-line map versus a multi-touch audio-tactile interactive map (3M M2256PW + thermoformed overlay + SAPI TTS) with 24 blind adults, measuring efficiency, effectiveness, and SUS satisfaction plus 2-week delayed recall.
The interactive map is ~2.6x faster to learn and scores significantly higher on SUS (88.1 vs 79.5, p < .001) while spatial knowledge acquisition is equivalent across map types, and user confidence diverges from effectiveness after a 2-week delay — landmark knowledge decays ~45% while confidence does not.
For inclusive-cartography this paper supplies a rigorous empirical baseline for interactive tactile map design and provides direct evidence that self-reported confidence is a misleading proxy for retained spatial knowledge, relevant to the LLM-confidence threshold critique in the Wabiski cognitive review protocol.
