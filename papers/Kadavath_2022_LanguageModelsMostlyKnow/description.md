Empirical Anthropic study showing that large language models (up to 52B) are well-calibrated on multiple-choice and True/False tasks in favorable formats, that P(True) self-evaluation of their own free-form samples is useful but prompt-sensitive, and that a trained P(IK) value head can predict whether a model "knows" an answer and partially generalize across tasks. Key negative findings: calibration collapses under format changes ("none of the above," True/False reformulation), RLHF policies are miscalibrated but fixable via logit temperature rescaling, and P(IK) trained on one distribution becomes over- or under-confident on out-of-distribution tasks (GSM8k flip from underconfident to overconfident when training on a 4-task mixture). This is the canonical reference for the critique of Wabiski 2026's fixed LLM-confidence thresholds (>0.90 auto-include, 0.70–0.90 human review): Kadavath demonstrates mechanistically why a single confidence cutoff is unreliable across the format, prompt, and distribution shifts that a real screening pipeline inevitably encounters.
