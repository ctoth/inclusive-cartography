Holloway, Marriott, and Butler compare desktop-3D-printed relief map models against traditional swell-paper tactile graphics for use by blind and low-vision readers across pilot interviews, a within-subjects formal study with 16 BLV participants, and a follow-on interactive-audio-label campus-model evaluation with O&M trainers and BLV experts.
They report that 3D-printed maps yield significantly better identification accuracy, better mental-model formation, and faster feature-finding on matched park and train-station maps, are unanimously preferred by participants, and they present a conductive-PLA + capacitive Touch Board audio-label prototype along with nine actionable design guidelines for accessible 3D-printed cartography.
For this project the paper supplies quantitative evidence, a buildable low-cost pipeline, and rule-like guidelines that can directly feed into specifications and evaluation criteria for inclusive cartographic artefacts.
