# Abstract

## Original Text (Verbatim)

Tactile maps are widely used in Orientation and Mobility (O&M) training for people who are blind or have severe vision impairment to provide knowledge of their environment and to support navigation and route planning. They are also a primary way to present accessible graphics, however it is increasingly possible to present accessible graphics using 3D printers. Using cheap desktop 3D printers our advantages over tactile equivalents for 2D modes with advantages over tactile equivalents for the BLV readers. The findings inform the design of larger interactive 3D printed floor plans aiding navigation of both sighted and vision impaired visitors in public spaces such as train stations, shopping centres, museums or offices.

Our studies have significant implications for O&M training, suggesting that 3D prints augment or replace the current use of tactile maps. They also inform the design of professional guidelines for the use and design of accessible 3D prints and of teaching strategies for reading them (§Guidelines).

## Our Interpretation

The paper addresses a concrete open question in accessible cartography: do 3D-printed relief models of real-world maps actually outperform the long-standing practice of embossed / swell-paper tactile graphics for blind and low-vision readers, or are they just novelty? Using pilot interviews, a within-subjects study with 16 BLV participants on matched park and train-station maps, and a follow-on evaluation of an interactive capacitive-touch audio-label campus model with O&M trainers and BLV experts, the authors show that 3D prints give significantly better identification accuracy, better mental-model formation, faster feature-finding, and unanimous participant preference. The paper is directly relevant to the inclusive-cartography project because it supplies empirical effect sizes, a reproducible low-cost authoring pipeline (conductive PLA + Bare Conductive Touch Board), and nine actionable design guidelines.
