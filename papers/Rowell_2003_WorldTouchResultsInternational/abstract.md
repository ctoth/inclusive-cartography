# Abstract

## Original Text (Verbatim)

This paper reports the findings of an international survey that collected data on production methods, techniques, outputs and preferences among tactile map producers, designers and researchers worldwide. A number of constraints and inconsistencies are identified and we speculate on the extent to which the current state of tactile map production is likely to meet the needs of the client population. We describe the sample in which the report is based, the output in terms of quantity of maps produced, choice of production method, types of environment mapped and intended users of maps. We also focus on issues of map design, such as the choice of map size and format, and choice of symbols.

## Our Interpretation

Rowell and Ungar report the first international quantitative snapshot of tactile map production, based on a 94-respondent / 43-country questionnaire. They document which production technologies dominate in practice (microcapsule paper and thermoform/vacuum-forming), what kinds of maps are produced (mobility and geography lead), how producers rank the importance of different tactile symbol categories (point and line features above shading; Braille labels above scale bars), and conclude that the field lacks shared standards and would benefit from a common tactile design language. The paper is widely used as a foundational empirical citation for the state of tactile cartography at the turn of the century and as a baseline against which newer accessible map technologies are compared.
