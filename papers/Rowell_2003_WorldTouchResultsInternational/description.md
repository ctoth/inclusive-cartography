This paper reports results of an international questionnaire survey of 94 tactile map producers from 43 countries, conducted circa 2001-2002, quantifying which production technologies are used (microcapsule paper and thermoform/vacuum-forming dominate), which types of maps are produced (mobility and geography maps lead), and which tactile symbol categories producers rank as most important (point features and lines rank higher than area shading; Braille labels rank higher than scale bars or north arrows).
The authors document a field operating largely without shared standards, find a strong UK sampling bias, and argue for a shared tactile design language rather than a single rigid standard, situating their survey against earlier technology-only reviews like Tatham (1991) and Edman (1992).
For inclusive-cartography this is a foundational empirical baseline: ground-truth on which tactile production methods had real adoption, a producer-validated hierarchy of tactile symbol importance, and a widely cited reference point for the long-standing absence of international standards in tactile map design.
