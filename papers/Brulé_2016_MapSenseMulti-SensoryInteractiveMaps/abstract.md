# Abstract

## Original Text (Verbatim)

We report on the design process leading to the creation of MapSense, a multi-sensory interactive map for visually impaired children. We conducted a formative study in a specialized institute to understand children's educational needs, their context of care and their preferences regarding interactive technologies. The findings (1) outline the needs for tools and methods to help children acquire spatial skills and (2) provide four design guidelines for educational assistive technologies. Based on these findings and an iterative process, we designed and deployed MapSense in the institute during two days. It enables collaborations between children with a broad range of impairments, and facilitates the satisfaction of the former and eases the work of the latter.

---

## Our Interpretation

The paper tackles the absence of practical, classroom-ready interactive maps for visually impaired children by running a formative ethnographic study with 15 specialized caretakers and teachers, then iterating two prototypes (Mappie, MapSense) that combine a capacitive tablet, raised-paper figurative overlay, conductive ink, and 3D-printed tangible landmarks. The key findings are qualitative: VI children of mixed ages and impairments engage immediately with the multi-sensory setup, caretakers appropriate and extend the system far beyond geography, and the authors argue for teacher-level authoring, modular low-cost hardware, and explicit rejection of one-size-fits-all accessibility. For inclusive cartography this is direct prior art — it defines what an accessible child-facing map-literacy tool looks like in situ and supplies concrete design constraints (multi-sensory redundancy, caretaker-as-author, heterogeneity of disability) that any downstream inclusive-cartography system should respect.
