MapSense is a CHI 2016 design-research paper that builds and evaluates two interactive map prototypes (Mappie and MapSense) for visually impaired children by combining a consumer capacitive tablet, raised-paper figurative overlays, conductive-ink touch points, and 3D-printed modular tangible landmarks, grounded in a formative interview study with 15 specialized caretakers and teachers at the Institute for the Young Blind of Toulouse.
The authors report qualitative in-situ evaluations with ~19 VI children, show that children spontaneously adopt two-handed scanning and collaborative storytelling with the tangibles, that caretakers appropriate and extend the system far beyond geography lessons, and distill four design guidelines emphasizing multi-sensory redundancy, teacher-level authoring, low-cost D-I-Y hardware, and heterogeneity-aware design that refuses "one-size-fits-all" accessibility.
For the inclusive-cartography project this paper is a direct, high-relevance exemplar of participatory inclusive map design, strong counter-evidence to any framing that reduces accessible cartography to screen-reader-over-Leaflet, and a concrete source of design requirements (multi-sensory encoding, teacher authoring, modular tangibles, caretaker-as-stakeholder) that should shape both the review's scoping and its quality-assessment criteria.
