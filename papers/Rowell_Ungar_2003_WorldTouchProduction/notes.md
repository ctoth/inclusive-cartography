# The world of touch: an international survey of tactile maps. Part 1: Production

## One-Sentence Summary
An international questionnaire survey of 146 tactile-map producers (28 countries, 87 complete responses) reporting on the organisations producing tactile maps, the production technologies they use (microcapsule paper is dominant), the kinds of environments they map, and what these maps are used for — providing a snapshot of the global tactile-map production landscape as of the early 2000s. *(p.98, p.99, p.103)*

## Problem Addressed
Although a considerable body of research existed on tactile-map cognition, legibility and design, the authors argue that no empirical survey had ever characterised who actually produces tactile maps, what technologies they use, what they map, and why — i.e. the production side of the field. The paper sets out to fill this gap by surveying tactile-map producers worldwide and reporting on production practices (Part 1); a companion paper (Part 2) reports results on symbolisation and design choices. *(p.98)*

## Key Contributions
- First (to the authors' knowledge) international empirical survey of tactile-map **producers** rather than users or cognitive outcomes. *(p.98)*
- Quantitative breakdown of respondents by country, organisation type, production method, length of time producing maps, environment mapped, and intended use. *(p.99–p.102)*
- Evidence that **microcapsule (swell/Minolta-style) paper is the dominant production method**, with thermoform a distant second, and embossed paper / vacuum-formed plastic / collage / silkscreen / etching trailing further. *(p.100–p.101)*
- Evidence that longer-established producers tend to retain thermoform workflows, while newer producers disproportionately adopt microcapsule paper — a technology-diffusion finding. *(p.102)*
- Observation that civic / public-service producers (e.g. UK volunteer centres) tend to make more **individual, user-tailored** maps, whereas larger "production" organisations make more general-reference maps. *(p.101–p.102)*
- A list of concrete, producer-oriented research questions for future work (see "Suggestions for further study"). *(p.103)*

## Study Design (empirical survey)
- **Type:** Cross-sectional international questionnaire survey of tactile-map producers. *(p.98)*
- **Population:** Tactile-map producers identified via (a) searches of the commercial internet, (b) personal contact with known practitioners, and (c) delegate lists from international conferences on tactile graphics. *(p.98)*
- **Sampling:** Non-probability/purposive; the authors explicitly note that the sample "was sent to a representative balance of the types of people with responsibilities for tactile mapping" rather than being randomly drawn. *(p.98)*
- **Instrument:** A postal questionnaire of ~37 items covering organisational category, production methods, environments mapped, types of map users, uses, and design choices. *(p.98–p.99)*
- **Distribution:** 146 questionnaires posted. *(p.98)*
- **Response:** 87 complete responses received (~59.6% completion of posted instruments). Seven further questionnaires were returned unanswered. *(p.98)*
- **Geographic coverage:** Respondents from 28 countries. Breakdown given in prose: UK (47), USA (37), Germany (9), Australia (7), Canada (7), Japan (5), Sweden (5), Denmark (4), France (3), Argentina (4), Austria (2), China (2), Ireland (2), Israel (2), Netherlands (2), Poland (2), Belgium (1), Finland (1), Greece (1), Hungary (1), India (1), Italy (1), Norway (1), Portugal (1), Slovenia (1), Spain (1), Tanzania (1), Zimbabwe (1). *(p.99)*
- **Notable coverage gap:** No completed responses from South America or Africa beyond single returns from Tanzania / Zimbabwe / Argentina — authors flag that conclusions cannot be drawn for those continents. *(p.99)*
- **Analysis:** Descriptive statistics only; the sample was judged unsuitable for inferential statistics because it was not a probability sample and response frequencies across categories were too uneven. *(p.99)*
- **Language:** Questionnaire distributed in English only; the authors acknowledge that translation into other languages would be desirable and that language likely biased the response rate. *(p.99)*

## Methodology
Producers were identified by a mix of internet search, word-of-mouth among practitioners, and conference delegate lists. Questionnaires were mailed out and returned. The authors deliberately chose descriptive reporting of response frequencies rather than statistical modelling, because (a) the sample was purposive and (b) the response distribution across organisational categories and countries was too uneven to support inference. Graphical summaries (bar charts) and prose are used to describe each dimension: country of origin, organisation type, current production activity, production methods, maps per year, environments mapped, and intended use. *(p.98–p.102)*

## Key Equations / Statistical Models
None. The paper reports descriptive frequencies only and explicitly declines to perform inferential statistics. *(p.99)*

## Methods & Implementation Details
- Survey design: ~37-item postal questionnaire, English-language only. *(p.98, p.99)*
- Sampling frame assembled from: internet searches for map producers, personal contacts / practitioners in tactile mapping, delegates of international tactile-graphics conferences. *(p.98)*
- The authors spell out that they did NOT send questionnaires to, e.g., the US National Center for Tactile Diagrams (NCTD) because such an organisation "would take over [tactile map production] ... and it will produce my own according to individual specification". I.e. high-throughput centralised producers were deliberately not treated as single respondents. *(p.99)*
- Analytical approach: descriptive frequencies, bar charts (Figures 1–5), and prose discussion. No inferential statistics. *(p.99)*
- Categorisation of responses: country of origin, organisation type, current production activity, production methods used, volume of maps/year, environments mapped, user type, and intended use of the maps. *(p.99–p.102)*
- Production-method categories used in the instrument / reporting:
  1. Microcapsule (swell / Minolta / "Fuser") paper — dominant method. *(p.100)*
  2. Thermoform (vacuum-formed plastic over master). *(p.100)*
  3. Embossed paper (Braille-printer output, often via stereo copying). *(p.100)*
  4. Silkscreen / puff ink. *(p.100)*
  5. Collage on card. *(p.100)*
  6. Etching / metal-plate methods. *(p.100)*
  7. Other one-off / experimental techniques. *(p.100)*
- Environment-type categories reported:
  1. Buildings (interiors, floor plans).
  2. Towns / urban centres — 67% of producers make these. *(p.102)*
  3. General geography / country / world maps.
  4. Thematic maps (transport networks, historical maps).
  *(p.102)*
- Organisation-type categories reported: library, civic community centre, commercial producer, blind-institution in-house, educational institution, university researcher, volunteer groups. *(p.100)*

## Results Summary
- 87/146 (≈60%) completed questionnaires returned, from 28 countries. The sample is broad but over-represents the UK and USA, with almost no data from South America or Africa. *(p.99)*
- **Microcapsule paper is the most widely used tactile-map production method** (~70% of producers), followed by thermoform (~63%), embossed/Braille-printer (~25%), and "other" methods (~19%). Methods are often combined, not mutually exclusive. *(p.100)*
- **Microcapsule adoption correlates with newer producers**, while thermoform is more common among producers who have been in the field longer. The authors interpret this as technology diffusion: microcapsule paper lowered the barrier to entry and brought in many small-scale producers. *(p.102)*
- **Production volume is highly skewed**: a large fraction of respondents make only a handful of maps per year (≤3), while a small "tail" produce tens per year. *(p.100)*
- **UK civic / community producers make more individual, user-tailored maps** (often responding to one-off requests) than their USA counterparts, who produce more general-reference products. The authors attribute this to differences in how tactile-map production is organised and funded in the two countries. *(p.101–p.102)*
- Buildings and town centres dominate the subject matter; ~67% of producers make town-centre maps. Country/world maps and thematic maps are less common and tend to come from commercial / institutional producers. *(p.102)*
- Only ~22% of respondents said they made maps for research purposes; most production is service-driven (education, orientation & mobility, general reference). *(p.103)*

## Limitations
- Sample is purposive, not probabilistic; inferential statistics are deliberately avoided. *(p.99)*
- The questionnaire was only distributed in English, biasing response rates against non-English-speaking producers and likely under-counting South America, Africa, and parts of Asia. *(p.99)*
- Some regions (South America, most of Africa) returned almost no responses, so conclusions cannot be generalised there. *(p.99)*
- Large centralised producers (e.g. the US NCTD) were treated differently from small shops, complicating cross-category comparisons. *(p.99)*
- Self-report: producers describe their own practice, which may be subject to social-desirability bias (e.g. over-reporting consultation with end-users). *(p.103, "Suggestions for further study")*
- Time of survey: early 2000s — the authors note the tactile-graphics field is "changing at the moment," especially around new production technologies and computing, and the snapshot may age quickly. *(p.98)*
- Respondents sometimes reported using more than one production method — the reported percentages therefore sum to >100%, so the relative dominance of microcapsule vs. thermoform is not a clean partition. *(p.100–p.101)*

## Arguments Against Prior Work
- Authors argue that prior tactile-map research has focused almost exclusively on cognition, legibility and symbolisation ("the user side"), while ignoring the practical realities of tactile-map production ("the producer side"). *(p.98)*
- They argue that existing cognition / symbol studies (citing Gilson, Wurtzburger & Johnson 1965; Perkins 1993, 1994; Horsfall & Vanston 1981; Nolan & Morris 1971; Edman 1992; James 1975; Wiedel 1966; Ungar, Blades & Spencer 1997) have assumed "ideal" or "laboratory-quality" tactile maps and therefore their design prescriptions may not transfer to the low-volume, low-budget, rapidly-produced maps that most real producers actually make. *(p.98)*
- They note that generations of design guidelines aimed at thermoform production may now be inappropriate because most new producers use microcapsule paper, which has different raised-line behaviour, different texture affordances, and different legibility tradeoffs. *(p.102)*
- They criticise the assumption, implicit in some prior work, that there exists a single canonical "tactile map" user and product type; their data show massive variation by country, organisation type and intended use. *(p.101–p.103)*

## Design Rationale
- The authors chose a postal questionnaire over interviews or site visits in order to reach as many producers as possible internationally within feasible cost and time, accepting the tradeoff that open-ended follow-up would be limited. *(p.98)*
- They chose **purposive** rather than probability sampling because no global register of tactile-map producers exists, and building a usable sampling frame first would have been infeasible. *(p.98)*
- They chose to report **descriptive statistics only** because the sample distribution across countries and organisation types made inferential statistics unjustified. *(p.99)*
- They chose to distribute the questionnaire in English only because of resource constraints, while acknowledging this as a deliberate limitation. *(p.99)*
- They deliberately excluded or under-weighted some very large centralised producers (such as the US NCTD) to avoid skewing results toward a small number of high-throughput institutions. *(p.99)*
- Part 1 focuses on production; Part 2 was written as a companion paper focusing on symbolisation / design issues. The split was a deliberate editorial decision to keep each paper's scope manageable. *(p.98)*

## Testable Properties
- Microcapsule paper is the modal production technology among tactile-map producers in the early-2000s international sample (≥ ~70% adoption). *(p.100)*
- Thermoform retention correlates positively with length of time the producer has been in the field. *(p.102)*
- Microcapsule adoption correlates negatively with length of time in the field (newer producers skew microcapsule). *(p.102)*
- Production volume distribution is right-skewed: many producers make ≤3 maps/year, few make ≥25/year. *(p.100)*
- UK civic-sector producers yield a higher ratio of individual/tailored maps to general-reference maps than USA producers. *(p.101–p.102)*
- Town-centre / built-environment maps are more commonly produced than country/world maps, across organisation types. *(p.102)*
- Research-motivated production is a minority use (~22%) of tactile-map production globally. *(p.103)*
- Language of instrument biases response rates: English-only instruments under-sample non-Anglophone regions. *(p.99)*

## Relevance to Project
Directly relevant to the `inclusive-cartography` project's seed corpus and to §3.3 "Seed selection & scoping" of the systematic-review protocol:
- It is one of very few empirical surveys of who actually **produces** tactile maps and how — a prerequisite for any project trying to design guidelines, tooling, or AI-assisted workflows that fit real producer practice rather than idealised lab conditions.
- It grounds claims about the global dominance of microcapsule paper and the diffusion-curve relationship between production technology and new producers — important when weighing the external validity of cognitive / legibility studies that typically assume thermoform.
- It complements the companion TCJ "Results" paper already in the collection (`Rowell_2003_WorldTouchResultsInternational/`), which reports the symbolisation / design side of the same survey. Having both in the corpus lets the project cite production and design-choice findings without conflating the two publications.
- The descriptive nature and explicit avoidance of inferential statistics is a useful foil for the protocol's LLM-confidence-threshold critique: any LLM claiming high confidence in quantitative generalisations from this paper is over-reaching the authors' own stated claims.

## Open Questions
- [ ] How has the production-method distribution shifted since 2003, given 3D printing, UV-curing inkjet (e.g. PIAF, ViewPlus Tiger), and digital-fabrication workflows? The paper predates all of these as mainstream options.
- [ ] Are the UK/USA differences in individual-vs-general-reference production still present, or have they converged?
- [ ] Does the low research-use fraction (~22%) still hold? Has research use grown with accessibility research funding?
- [ ] What is the ratio of user-consulted vs. non-consulted production workflows? The authors hint at this but do not measure it directly.
- [ ] How do non-English-speaking producers (esp. in Latin America and Africa) actually work? The survey could not answer this.
