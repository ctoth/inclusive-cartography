# Papers Index

## Brock_2015_InteractiveMapsUsability  (accessibility, tactile-maps, visually-impaired, usability-study, audio-tactile)
Brock et al. present the first controlled within-subjects usability comparison of a classical braille-legend raised-line map versus a multi-touch audio-tactile interactive map (3M M2256PW + thermoformed overlay + SAPI TTS) with 24 blind adults, measuring efficiency, effectiveness, and SUS satisfaction plus 2-week delayed recall.
The interactive map is ~2.6x faster to learn and scores significantly higher on SUS (88.1 vs 79.5, p < .001) while spatial knowledge acquisition is equivalent across map types, and user confidence diverges from effectiveness after a 2-week delay — landmark knowledge decays ~45% while confidence does not.
For inclusive-cartography this paper supplies a rigorous empirical baseline for interactive tactile map design and provides direct evidence that self-reported confidence is a misleading proxy for retained spatial knowledge, relevant to the LLM-confidence threshold critique in the Wabiski cognitive review protocol.

## Brulé_2016_MapSenseMulti-SensoryInteractiveMaps  (accessibility, inclusive-cartography, tactile-maps, visually-impaired, participatory-design)
MapSense is a CHI 2016 design-research paper that builds and evaluates two interactive map prototypes (Mappie and MapSense) for visually impaired children by combining a consumer capacitive tablet, raised-paper figurative overlays, conductive-ink touch points, and 3D-printed modular tangible landmarks, grounded in a formative interview study with 15 specialized caretakers and teachers at the Institute for the Young Blind of Toulouse.
The authors report qualitative in-situ evaluations with ~19 VI children, show that children spontaneously adopt two-handed scanning and collaborative storytelling with the tangibles, that caretakers appropriate and extend the system far beyond geography lessons, and distill four design guidelines emphasizing multi-sensory redundancy, teacher-level authoring, low-cost D-I-Y hardware, and heterogeneity-aware design that refuses "one-size-fits-all" accessibility.
For the inclusive-cartography project this paper is a direct, high-relevance exemplar of participatory inclusive map design, strong counter-evidence to any framing that reduces accessible cartography to screen-reader-over-Leaflet, and a concrete source of design requirements (multi-sensory encoding, teacher authoring, modular tangibles, caretaker-as-stakeholder) that should shape both the review's scoping and its quality-assessment criteria.

## Ducasse_2018_AccessibleInteractiveMapsVisually  (accessibility, tactile-maps, interactive-maps, visually-impaired, spatial-cognition, hci)
This Springer chapter surveys three decades of accessible interactive map prototypes for visually impaired users and proposes a two-level classification splitting devices into Digital Interactive Maps (DIMs, flat touchscreens / haptic devices) and Hybrid Interactive Maps (HIMs, which add a physical/refreshable tactile layer), then compares families on cost, map content, readability and interactivity.
Key findings: HIMs consistently outperform pure DIMs on shape recognition and layout learning, audio labels beat braille for label-dense maps, absolute-input devices beat relative ones, and interactive tactile maps (e.g. Brock et al. 2015) produce measurable spatial-cognition gains that transfer to real environments, while refreshable pin displays remain too small, slow and expensive for practical field use.
Directly relevant to inclusive-cartography: establishes the canonical DIM/HIM taxonomy and empirical base against which any LLM-driven or audio-only cartographic surrogate must justify itself, especially regarding persistent reference frames, multi-finger exploration, and authoring by VI users themselves.

## Götzelmann_2016_LucentMaps3DPrintedAudiovisual  (tactile-maps, 3d-printing, accessibility, audiovisual-feedback, capacitive-touch)
LucentMaps presents a pipeline for producing translucent FDM-3D-printed tactile map overlays in PLA that bond to a consumer smartphone or tablet and embed passive "CapCode" capacitive identification markers so the device auto-identifies which map is sitting on it and can deliver per-feature speech, category filtering, and route guidance via a standard Android app backed by OpenStreetMap.
A small n=5 qualitative study with blind and visually impaired participants near Nuremberg demonstrated feasibility at ~$0.40 of filament and ~1 h print time per ~30×30 cm overlay on a ~$500 consumer printer, with users successfully identifying maps, locating features, and following routes; residual-vision users additionally benefited from the tablet screen shining through the translucent print.
For the inclusive-cartography review, the paper is a canonical 2016 cost/feasibility datum for end-user producible tactile maps and directly informs the review's third research question on tools and technologies for transferable, low-cost accessible cartography.

## Gual_2015_EffectVolumetric3DTactile  (inclusive-cartography, accessibility, tactile-maps, 3d-printing, empirical-study)
A counterbalanced within-subjects field study (N=16 across blind, visually-impaired, and sighted-blindfolded profiles) comparing a flat microcapsule (swell-paper) tactile map against a mixed map whose 10 point symbols are replaced with FDM-printed ABS volumetric symbols 3-8 mm tall, using three wayfinding tasks timed and scored for errors. The 3D-enhanced map reduces mean location time and error counts for every profile with the largest gains for blind users, closes the performance gap between visual profiles, and shows a transfer-learning advantage for participants who start on the 3D map. For the inclusive-cartography project this is a primary empirical data point that hybrid "inclusive" map production (swell paper + 3D-printed point symbols) improves BVI wayfinding, and a useful small-N replication target for the systematic review's claim-extraction pipeline.

## Holloway_2018_AccessibleMapsBlindComparing  (tactile-maps, 3d-printing, accessibility, blind-low-vision, user-study)
Holloway, Marriott, and Butler compare desktop-3D-printed relief map models against traditional swell-paper tactile graphics for use by blind and low-vision readers across pilot interviews, a within-subjects formal study with 16 BLV participants, and a follow-on interactive-audio-label campus-model evaluation with O&M trainers and BLV experts.
They report that 3D-printed maps yield significantly better identification accuracy, better mental-model formation, and faster feature-finding on matched park and train-station maps, are unanimously preferred by participants, and they present a conductive-PLA + capacitive Touch Board audio-label prototype along with nine actionable design guidelines for accessible 3D-printed cartography.
For this project the paper supplies quantitative evidence, a buildable low-cost pipeline, and rule-like guidelines that can directly feed into specifications and evaluation criteria for inclusive cartographic artefacts.

## Kadavath_2022_LanguageModelsMostlyKnow  (llm-calibration, ai-assisted-screening, self-evaluation, honesty, anthropic)
Empirical Anthropic study showing that large language models (up to 52B) are well-calibrated on multiple-choice and True/False tasks in favorable formats, that P(True) self-evaluation of their own free-form samples is useful but prompt-sensitive, and that a trained P(IK) value head can predict whether a model "knows" an answer and partially generalize across tasks. Key negative findings: calibration collapses under format changes ("none of the above," True/False reformulation), RLHF policies are miscalibrated but fixable via logit temperature rescaling, and P(IK) trained on one distribution becomes over- or under-confident on out-of-distribution tasks (GSM8k flip from underconfident to overconfident when training on a 4-task mixture). This is the canonical reference for the critique of Wabiski 2026's fixed LLM-confidence thresholds (>0.90 auto-include, 0.70-0.90 human review): Kadavath demonstrates mechanistically why a single confidence cutoff is unreliable across the format, prompt, and distribution shifts that a real screening pipeline inevitably encounters.

## Lin_2022_TeachingModelsExpressUncertainty  (llm-calibration, verbalized-uncertainty, distribution-shift, gpt-3, finetuning)
Shows that GPT-3 can be supervised-finetuned on the CalibratedMath benchmark to emit a verbalized confidence estimate (numeric percentage or categorical word) alongside each answer, with the confidence remaining moderately calibrated under content and format distribution shift.
Verbalized confidence outperforms a temperature-scaled answer-logit baseline on format-shifted (Multi-answer) evaluation but loses to it on raw MSE for content-shifted (Multiply-divide) evaluation, and is only weakly correlated with answer-token logits, establishing that verbalized confidence is a distinct learned signal rather than a reparameterization of logits.
Directly relevant to inclusive-cartography's critique of Wabinski 2026's confidence-threshold rule: the paper quantifies how much (or little) a non-finetuned LM's self-reported confidence can be trusted, shows few-shot prompting does not substitute for finetuning, and gives the concrete MSE/MAD numbers (best-case ~15-22% calibration error on a synthetic arithmetic benchmark) that upper-bound what any in-context review protocol can expect.

## Palivcová_2020_InteractiveTactileMapTool  (tactile-maps, accessibility, older-adults, indoor-navigation, user-study)
Palivcová, Macík, and Míkovec present an interactive 3D-printed tactile map tailored to visually impaired older adults living in a residential care home, combining haptically salient landmark objects, large capacitive touch-sensitive room/corridor segments, and an audio route-guidance mode produced through three iterative prototype generations.
A qualitative in-situ evaluation with 10 VI older adults (mean age 81.4, 5F/5M, 62-87) indicates the design helps users acquire both survey and route knowledge of the indoor environment, while surfacing concrete guidelines on map scale (~1 cm/1 m), tactile symbol distinctness, audio rate/volume adjustability, and ergonomic stability.
Directly relevant to inclusive cartography: it is a scarce empirical data point on the intersection of aging and visual impairment, provides a reusable indoor tactile-map design pattern, and illustrates why LLM-confidence thresholds that weigh statistical rigor alone would underrate small-N formative work with high topical value.

## Papadopoulos_2018_OrientationMobilityAidsIndividuals  (blindness, orientation-mobility, audio-tactile-maps, verbal-description, empirical-study)
A within-subjects experiment (N=20 totally blind adults) comparing a structured audio-only verbal description of three real urban areas in Thessaloniki to an embossed audio-tactile map of the same areas for O&M pre-journey preparation. Participants produced significantly more correct answers about routes and points of interest when using the audio-tactile map (M=5.75, SD=1.37) than when using the verbal description (M=4.85, SD=1.31), with no significant difference in study time or execution time between conditions. The paper provides one of the few direct, ecologically-valid benchmarks of non-visual encoding channels for cognitive map formation and is a foundational data point for any inclusive-cartography system that needs to predict accuracy gains when moving from audio-only to tactile-plus-audio representations.

## Perkins_2002_CartographyProgressTactileMapping  (tactile-maps, inclusive-cartography, accessibility, literature-review, wayfinding)
Perkins' first progress report on cartography for Progress in Human Geography surveys post-Fleming (1990) tactile-mapping research across six themes: cognitive map use, design, standardization, production technology, technological substitutes (GIS/GPS/audio/VR), and ethics. Key findings: vacuum-forming and microcapsule hard copy still dominate despite 25+ years of electronic mobility aid development, no international tactile-symbol standard has emerged, and research is overwhelmingly sighted-led, western, urban, and academy-based on an inappropriate medical model of disability. Provides the canonical framing and critical lens for this project's inclusive-cartography work, anchoring the ethics critique that any confidence-threshold or seed-selection protocol should address.

## Rowell_2003_WorldTouchResultsInternational  (tactile-maps, accessibility, survey, cartography, visual-impairment)
This paper reports results of an international questionnaire survey of 94 tactile map producers from 43 countries, conducted circa 2001-2002, quantifying which production technologies are used (microcapsule paper and thermoform/vacuum-forming dominate), which types of maps are produced (mobility and geography maps lead), and which tactile symbol categories producers rank as most important (point features and lines rank higher than area shading; Braille labels rank higher than scale bars or north arrows).
The authors document a field operating largely without shared standards, find a strong UK sampling bias, and argue for a shared tactile design language rather than a single rigid standard, situating their survey against earlier technology-only reviews like Tatham (1991) and Edman (1992).
For inclusive-cartography this is a foundational empirical baseline: ground-truth on which tactile production methods had real adoption, a producer-validated hierarchy of tactile symbol importance, and a widely cited reference point for the long-standing absence of international standards in tactile map design.

## Taylor_2016_Customizable3DPrintedTactile  (tactile-maps, 3d-printing, accessibility, capacitive-overlay, openstreetmap)
Presents an end-to-end system that turns an OpenStreetMap-specified location into a customizable STL, printed on a consumer FDM machine with a non-conductive PLA base and conductive PLA raised features, and then used as a passive capacitive overlay on an unmodified Android tablet whose companion app speaks an audio label whenever a raised feature is touched.
Contributions: a web authoring interface (simple + advanced), a four-corner rigid registration scheme coupling print to tablet, the conductive-PLA touchscreen overlay mechanism, a feasibility study with 12 visually impaired participants across three cities that surfaces real failure modes (intermittent capacitive touch, small-feature misses, roads printed too thin) and explicit design directions for the next iteration.
Directly relevant to inclusive-cartography's scope because it is a clean reference point for commodity-hardware inclusive map production and a useful counter-example for protocol-level LLM-confidence thresholds: the architecture is confidently described while the physical-layer reliability is entirely uncharacterized.

## Tian_2023_JustAskCalibrationStrategies  (llm-calibration, rlhf, verbalized-uncertainty, confidence-elicitation, prompting)
Tian et al. evaluate "just ask" strategies — verbalized numeric probabilities, linguistic likelihood expressions, and top-k with per-answer confidences — for eliciting calibrated confidence from RLHF-fine-tuned language models (GPT-4, ChatGPT, Claude-1/2, Llama-2-70B-chat) on TriviaQA, SciQ, and TruthfulQA. They find that verbalized probabilities, particularly in a two-step protocol, achieve roughly 50% lower Expected Calibration Error than the models' own conditional token probabilities or P(True) self-evaluation, requiring no fine-tuning, labeled calibration data, or logit access. The result is directly relevant to this project's critique of Wabinski 2026's LLM-confidence-threshold screening rule: it bounds how good and how bad verbalized confidences from RLHF chatbots can be, and supplies concrete prompt-level fixes (top-k, two-step elicitation) that Wabinski's protocol could adopt.

## Wabinski_2022_GuidelinesStandardizingTactileMaps  (tactile-maps, accessibility, inclusive-cartography, design-guidelines, review)
Wabinski, Moscicka and Touya review decades of empirical research and practitioner best-practice documents to consolidate measurable design guidelines for tactile maps — covering symbol inventories, minimum dimensions/spacings/heights, generalization rules, and how production method (thermoforming, swell-paper, braille embosser, 3D printing) constrains symbol choice.
They extract concrete parameter tables (Tables 2-4 on p.12) and a category-organized appendix of recommended, empirically distinguishable symbols (Appendix A, pp.18-20), and explicitly argue that no global tactile-map design standard yet exists despite a mature evidence base from Nolan & Morris, Wiedel & Groves, BANA/CBA, NSW, and ISO.
This paper is the inclusive-cartography project's single best reference for the numeric invariants any automated tactile-map pipeline must respect and directly motivates the 2026 systematic-review protocol authored by the same lead author.

## Wabiski_2026_CognitiveReviewProtocol  (systematic-review, inclusive-cartography, protocol, accessibility, ai-assisted-screening)
A formal, AI-assisted systematic-review protocol that applies PRISMA 2020, the Cochrane Handbook, and PICO to inclusive cartographic design for users with diverse perceptual, cognitive, and age-related abilities. It specifies a three-question research agenda (design principles, technologies/tools/media, and transferability), a cross-database boolean search strategy verified against PRESS 2015 by GPT-5 and Gemini 2.5, an ASReview + LLM screening pipeline gated by confidence thresholds (>0.90 auto-include, 0.70-0.90 human review), and a 14-item weighted quality checklist with a ≥70%-of-max inclusion floor. This IS the governing plan for the `inclusive-cartography` project — downstream claim extraction, stance reconciliation, and synthesis tooling in the repo should map directly onto §3.4-§3.8 of this document.

## Xiong_2024_LLMUncertaintyConfidenceElicitation  (llm-confidence, calibration, uncertainty-estimation, black-box-evaluation, benchmark)
Xiong et al. define a black-box framework for eliciting confidence from closed-source LLMs, decomposed into prompting, sampling, and aggregation strategies, and benchmark it on five LLMs across eight QA datasets.
The paper shows that vanilla verbalized confidence is severely overconfident (values cluster 80-100% in multiples of 5), that CoT/Top-K prompting reduces ECE mainly by raising accuracy rather than improving discrimination, and that Self-Random sampling with M=5 plus Avg-Conf or Pair-Rank aggregation is the most reliable configuration; the white-box/black-box AUROC gap is narrow (~0.522 to ~0.605).
This provides the empirical foundation for the Wabiski protocol LLM-confidence critique in inclusive-cartography: raw verbalized confidence is not trustworthy, ECE alone is an insufficient gate, and professional-knowledge domains defeat all current calibration methods.
